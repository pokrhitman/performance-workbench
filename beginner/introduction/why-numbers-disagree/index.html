Why Numbers Disagree

Picture one portfolio, one reporting period and two people looking at exactly the same underlying data — no missing transactions, no valuation gap, nothing wrong with the numbers themselves. And yet they walk away with two different performance figures and both of them are right.

This isn't a hypothetical. It's a conversation that plays out constantly between two roles.

The fund manager is being measured against a benchmark. Clients move money in and out of the portfolio on their own schedule — that's not something the manager controls and it's not something the benchmark knows or cares about either. So the manager needs a return that pretends every dollar was invested from day one, stripped of the timing and size of anyone's individual contributions or withdrawals. Only then is the comparison against the benchmark fair.

The financial controller isn't comparing anything to a benchmark. She wants to know how the capital actually deployed in this portfolio performed — the real dollars, arriving and leaving at the real moments they did. A return that quietly erases the timing of those cash flows would hide exactly the thing she's trying to understand: did the money, as it actually moved, make money?

Neither of them is wrong and neither of them made a calculation error. They're answering two genuinely different questions with the same underlying data and each question has its own correct method. Every method covered in this workbench — across every tier — exists because some real question needed an honest answer and "which question are you actually asking?" turns out to matter more than any formula.

Before we name any of those methods, it's worth seeing just how much a single, easy-to-overlook detail — the timing of one contribution — can matter, even before you've picked a method at all. That's the Interactive tab.

Below is one portfolio: a starting value, a single contribution and an ending value. Drag the slider to change only the day that contribution arrived — everything else stays fixed.

Start Value $100,000
Contribution +$10,000
End Value $119,000
Naive Return 19.00%
Day 1 Day 30

Notice the naive return above never changes, no matter where you drag the slider. It's only ever comparing the start and the end numbers — it has no idea when the $10,000 showed up, or how long it was actually exposed to the market before the period closed.

  • Performance figures can legitimately differ across two people looking at the same, correct data — that's a method choice, not a data error.
  • A benchmark-relative view (the fund manager's need) wants cash-flow timiing removed from the number entirely.
  • A capital-flow view (the controller's need) wants cash-flow timing built into the number.
  • A naive start-vs-end return is blind to cash-flow timing either way — it isn't either of these methods, and shouldn't be mistaken for one.
  • Later tiers name and build these methods precisely: Time-Weighted Return, Money-Weighted Return, Modified Dietz and the German BVI method based on a fund's NAV price.
