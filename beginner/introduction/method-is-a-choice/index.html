Method is a Choice, Not a Fact

By now the shape of the problem should feel familiar: two people, the same portfolio, both correct (page one) — because a return's two ingredients, capital and time, stop being obvious the moment money moves mid-period (page two). What's still missing is the practical question: given a real portfolio in front of you, how do you actually decide which method to use?

It's not a lookup. It's three questions, asked in order.

1. Do you have a valuation at every point money moved, or only a start value, an end value and the dates cash flowed? Some data sets have a full history — a valuation on every single date a conntribution or withdrawal happened. Others don't; all you've got is where the period started, where it ended and a list of dates and amounts in between. If it's the latter, you can still get a close approximation without needing that full history — that's the Modified Dietz method's entire reason to exist.

2- Are you reading the return off a published price per fund unit, or off portfolio value? Retail fund reporting is usually built on published Net Asset Value (NAV) per unit — a price that already has the fund's own running costs baked in. When that's what you're working from, the calculation is a fund-unit return: chain those published unit prices directly, with no cash-flow adjustment needed, because new units are simply issued or redeemed at whatever the prevailing price already is. Exactly which costs stay baked into that published price differs by market — Germany's BVI convention and the United States' SEC-standardized return are two examples, both covered in the Intermediate tier.

3. Who actually controls when the cash moved — the investor, or the fund itself? This is the question that splits the two remaining methods. When clients can move money in and out on their own schedule — an open-ended, liquid fund — that timing has nothing to do with the manager's skill, so a fair comparison against a benchmark needs it stripped out entirely. That's Timme-Weighted Return. But when the fund itself controls timing — a closed-end structure, a private equity commitment, a property fund where the investor can't simply withdraw on demand — the timing is part of the investor's real experience and erasing it would hide something true. That's what Money-Weighted Return (IRR) is built to capture instead.

Four methods, four different questions and every one of them can be the "right" answer — for a specific portfolio, with specific data, answering a specific question. That's the idea this entire Workbench is build around: not teaching you one formula to memorize, but teaching you to recognize which question is actually being asked.

Four methods, side by side. Each card shows what it needs and what it answers up front — click "Learn more" on any card for a short explanation of how it actually works.

Time-Weighted Return

Needs: a valuation at every cash-flow date

Answers: how did the manager perform, independent of client timing?

Splits the period into sub-periods at each cash flow, computes a return for each with the flow's effect removed, the chains them together. The standard method whenever a return needs to be fairly compared against a benchmark.

Money-Weighted Return (IRR)

Needs: only the dates and amounts of cash flows

Answers: how did the money actually invested perform, given it moved?

Solves for the single rate at which the present value of all cash flows nets to zero — no interim valuations required The natural fit for closed-end or illiquid structures where the investor doesn't control timing.

Modified Dietz

Needs: start value, end value and cash-flow dates

Answers: a close approximation of TWR without a full valuation history

Weights each cash flow by the fraction of the period it was actually invested for — a flow on day 1 counts almost fully, a flow on the last day barely counts at all. A practical middle ground when interim valuations aren't available.

Fund-Unit / NAV-Based Return

Needs: published fund unit values (NAV per unit)

Answers: how did an investor's fund units perform, at the price acutally published?

Chains returns straight from published unit prices, with no cash-flow adjustment — new units are simply issued or redeemed at the prevailing price. Fund-level costs are already baked in. Which investor-level costs stay excluded is set by national convention — the US SEC-standarized return and Germany's BVI method are two examples, covered in the Intermediate tier.

  • No fuill valuation history, only start/end values and cash-flow dates — use Modified Dietz.
  • Working from published fund unit prices (NAV) — use a fund-unit return; the exact national convention (US SEC-standardized, Germany's BVI, etc.) is covered in Intermediate.
  • Investor/client controls cash-flow timing, liquid fund, need a benchmark-fair comparison — use Time-Weighted Return.
  • Fund controls cash-flow timing, illiquid or closed-end structure — use Money-Weighted Return (IRR).
  • None of these is more "correct" than another — each answers a different question about the same portfolio.
