What a Return Measures

Someone tells you their portfolio made $10,000 last year. Good news? You can't actually tell yet — not without knowing one more thing.

A dollar gain is real money, but on its own it says nothing about performance. $10,000 on a $20,000 portfolio is a spectacular result. $10,000 on a $2,000,000 portfolio barely move the needle. Same dollar figure, two completely different stories — because a return isn't a dollar amount at all. It's a ration: the gain, measured against the capital that was put at risk to produce it.

But the capital isn't the whole story either. A 10% return sounds indentical whether it was earned in one month or over five years — yet those represent wildly different rates of performance. Time is the other half of the equation. (Turning "10% in one month" and "10% in five years" into numbers you can actually compare is its own topic — annualizing — and it gets a full chapter of its own later in this tier.)

Put the two together and a return is really just answering one question: how efficiently did this specific amount of capital, over this speficic stretch of time, turn into a different amount of capital? Every return figure, no matter how it's calculated, ultimately rests on three ingredients — a starting amount, an ending amount and the time between them.

That sounds simple enough when nothing else happens during the period. BUt as soon as money moves in or out partway through — a contribution, a withdrawal — "the starting amount" and "the time" stop being obvious. Deciding exactly what counts as capital and when is precisely what separates Time-Weighted Return, Money-Weighted Return, Modified Dietz and the German BVI method from each other, later in this workbench. Before we get there, though, it's worth seeing — on a few simple examples with no cash flows at all — just how differently "the same" portfolio can look depending on whether you're reading the dollar gain or the percent return. That's the Interactive tab.

Four portfolios below, each with a starting value and an ending value and no cash flows in between — nothing to complicate the picture yet. Toggle between viewing them ranked by dollar gain and ranked by percent return.

  • Return = gain ÷ capital at risk, expressed as a ratio (a percentage).
  • The same percent return over different lengths of time isn't directly comparable — time matters as much as the ratio itself.
