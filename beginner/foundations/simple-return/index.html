Simple Return

Every return in this workbench traces back to the three ingredients named at the end of the last chapter — a starting amount, an ending amount and the time between them. Simple return, also called holding period return , is what you get when none of those three ingredients get complicated: one starting value, one ending value, nothing moving in or out in between, no need to slice the period into pieces.

Take a portfolio worth $50,000 at the start of the year and $52,340 at the end, with no contributions or withdrawals along the way. The dollar gain is easy — $2,340 — but the return asks a different question: how big was that gain relative to the money that produced it? $2,340 ÷ $50,000 = 0.0468, or 4.68%. That's the entire calculation: (End Value − Start Value) ÷ Start Value.

This figure goes by two names depending on who's saying it. Investment performance people tend to say simple return; accounting and reporting contexts more often say holding period return. Both mean exactly this calculation — if you see either term with no cash flows attached, this is the formula behind it.

The catch is right here there in the name; simple. It only works because nothing happened to the money in between — no contribution landed on day 12, no withdrawal went out on day 20. The moment either of those happens, "start value" stops meaning what you'd assume, because part of the ending value wasn't invested for the whole period at all. That's exactly the problem the next chapter, Cash Flows Break Simple Return, is about. Before we get there,though, try the formula on your own numbers — that's the Interactive tab.

Type any two numbers below — your own portfolio's actual start and end value, if you want to check something real. Nothing here is precomputed: every result recalculates the instant you change either number, using the same simpleReturn() function from the formula engine that will power every calculator in this workbench.

Start Value here is negative — think of of a margin or loan position that begins the period in debt. The return divides by the absolute value of Start Value, so its sign always matches whether the position actually improved or worsened, no matter which side of zero it started on.

  • Simple return (also called holding period return) = (End Value − Start Value) ÷ Start Value.
  • Assumes no cash flows during the period — a contribution or withdrawal partway through breaks this calculation, covered in the next chapter.
  • The formula engine's simpleReturn() returns a decimal (0.0468), not a percentage — multiply by 100 to display it as one.
  • "Simple return" and "holding period return" are the same calculation under two different names, depending on which corner of the industry is talking.
  • If Start Value is negative (a margin or loan position that starts the period in debt), the formula divides by its absolute value, not the signed value — otherwise a shrinking debt would show up a negative return and a growing debt as a postive one.
